As an Engineering Manager leading an SRE team, I'm often asked what I look for in candidates after they pass the technical interview.

The answer is rarely about Kubernetes, Terraform, cloud platforms, or specific tools.

What I'm really evaluating is ownership, decision-making, communication, and systems thinking.

This article explains the qualities I value most when interviewing engineers and how our team approaches reliability, automation, collaboration, and continuous improvement.

## I'm Less Interested in What You Know

By the time we talk, you've probably already demonstrated that you can do the job.

Someone has reviewed your experience.
Someone has evaluated your technical skills.
Someone has decided that you're capable of performing the role.

My role as an Engineering Manager is different.

I'm trying to understand how you think.

Because after you're hired, nobody will pay you for solving interview questions.

You'll be paid to make decisions.

You'll be paid to collaborate with people.

You'll be paid to improve systems that already exist.

And you'll be paid to make good trade-offs when there is no perfect answer.

Technology changes constantly.

The tools we use today may not be the tools we use two years from now.

What matters to me is not whether you've memorized every Kubernetes feature, cloud service, or monitoring tool.

What matters is how you approach problems.

When I ask about a difficult situation you've experienced, I'm not looking for a textbook answer.

I'm trying to understand:

- How do you behave under pressure?
- How do you deal with uncertainty?
- How do you react when something goes wrong?
- Do you take ownership?
- What did you learn from the experience?

Everybody has made mistakes.

Everybody has dealt with incidents.

What matters is whether you learned something from them.

## Automation Is Not Always the Answer

One of my favorite topics is automation.

Not because I think everything should be automated.

In fact, I don't.

Automation has a cost.

Maintenance has a cost.

Complexity has a cost.

A good engineer understands those costs and evaluates them against the expected benefit.

The question isn't whether something can be automated.

The question is whether it should be.

Sometimes a simple manual process performed twice a year is perfectly acceptable.

Sometimes a daily repetitive task is consuming valuable engineering time and should have been automated months ago.

What I care about is the reasoning behind the decision.

I want engineers who understand trade-offs and can explain them clearly.

## Reliability Is a People Problem

As engineers, we often like to think of reliability as a technical challenge.

Monitoring.

Alerting.

Infrastructure.

Automation.

Those things matter.

But many reliability problems are actually people problems.

How do you convince development teams to prioritize stability improvements?

How do you handle disagreements between teams?

How do you balance business priorities with operational risk?

How do you communicate concerns without creating friction?

The best engineers I've worked with understand that reliability is not something imposed on a team.

It's something achieved together.

Technical skills help you identify problems.

Communication skills help you solve them.

## What Do You Do With Technical Debt?

Imagine an old pipeline.

It's messy.

It's difficult to understand.

Nobody is particularly proud of it.

Yet it works.

Now what?

Many engineers immediately want to rewrite it.

Others refuse to touch it.

Both approaches can be dangerous.

What interests me is how you evaluate the situation.

Do you understand the risks?

Can you identify the most painful parts?

Can you improve it incrementally?

Can you explain why a complete rewrite is or isn't justified?

Engineering is full of imperfect systems.

The ability to improve them responsibly is far more valuable than the ability to criticize them.

## What Happens When You Are On Call?

Eventually something breaks.

It always does.

The interesting question isn't whether you've experienced incidents.

The interesting question is what happens next.

Do you panic?

Do you communicate?

Do you gather information before acting?

Do you understand the business impact?

Do you focus on restoring service first and analyzing root causes later?

Do you learn from the incident after it's over?

Technical knowledge helps during incidents.

Good judgment is what makes the difference.

## There Are No Kingdoms

One thing I care deeply about is shared ownership.

I don't like environments where knowledge is hoarded, projects become personal territory, or individuals become gatekeepers.

I've seen teams where only one person understands a system.

Everything works until that person goes on vacation, changes teams, or leaves the company.

That's not expertise.

That's a risk.

In our team, everyone should understand what we're building and why we're building it.

Planning sessions are discussions, not presentations.

Daily meetings are not status reports.

They're opportunities to share context, ask questions, raise concerns, and help each other make better decisions.

Everyone is encouraged to contribute.

Everyone is encouraged to challenge ideas.

Everyone is encouraged to learn outside their immediate responsibilities.

Good teams don't create silos.

They create shared understanding.

We don't judge people for asking questions.

We don't expect people to know everything.

We expect people to be curious, engaged, and willing to learn.

The more ownership you take, the more context you gain.

The more context you gain, the better your decisions become.

And over time, that's how expertise is built.

Not by protecting knowledge.

By sharing it.

## Ownership Is More Important Than Expertise

If there's a common theme across all of these topics, it's ownership.

Ownership of systems.

Ownership of incidents.

Ownership of technical debt.

Ownership of team decisions.

Ownership of personal growth.

I can help someone learn a new tool.

I can help someone learn a new platform.

I can help someone understand a new technology.

What is much harder to teach is the habit of taking responsibility for outcomes.

The engineers who consistently stand out are not necessarily the smartest people in the room.

They're the people who care enough to improve things they weren't explicitly asked to improve.

They're the people who see a problem and take the initiative to solve it.

They're the people who think beyond the task in front of them.

## What I Look For

I look for engineers who ask:

- Why are we doing this?
- What problem are we actually solving?
- What are the trade-offs?
- What could go wrong?
- How will we maintain this six months from now?
- Is there a simpler approach?

I value ownership more than heroics.

I value curiosity more than confidence.

I value system thinking more than knowledge of a specific tool.

I value people who improve systems rather than simply work around their limitations.

Most importantly, I value engineers who continue to grow.

Technology changes.

Companies change.

Processes change.

The ability to learn, adapt, and improve never goes out of style.

If that sounds like the kind of environment where you would thrive, then we'll probably have a great conversation.
